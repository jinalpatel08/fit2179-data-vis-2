01 — Temperature

Australia is heating up

Since 1910, Australia's average annual temperature has risen by approximately 1.5°C — a dramatic shift in just over a century. The warming has accelerated sharply since the 1970s, and the most recent decade (2010–2020) is the hottest on record. Nine of the ten warmest years have occurred since 2005.

02 — By State

Where is it warming fastest?

The warming is not uniform across Australia. South Australia has recorded the highest temperature anomaly of any state at +1.32°C above the baseline, followed closely by the ACT at +1.28°C. Tasmania, buffered by the Southern Ocean, has experienced the least warming at +0.95°C — though this is still a significant departure from historical norms.

03 — Rainfall

The rains are shifting — and shrinking

Australia's rainfall is highly variable from year to year, but the long-term trend is unmistakable: dry years are becoming more frequent, and the driest years are getting drier. 2019 recorded just 275mm — the lowest annual total since records began in 1900, and well below the long-term average of 461mm. The 10-year rolling average has trended downward since the late 1990s.

04 — Water Storage

Melbourne's reservoirs under pressure

Melbourne's water storage levels tell a vivid story of climate impact on urban infrastructure. The Millennium Drought (1997–2009) drove combined reservoir storage to a historic low of 25.9% in June 2009, forcing the construction of the Wonthaggi desalination plant — a $3.5 billion infrastructure response to climate-driven water scarcity. 05 — Extreme Events

Extreme weather is becoming more frequent

As Australia's climate warms, the frequency and intensity of extreme weather events has increased markedly. Floods remain the most common disaster type, but wildfires have surged since the 2010s — culminating in the catastrophic 2019–20 Black Summer bushfire season. The east coast (NSW and Queensland) bears the greatest burden, accounting for more than half of all recorded events since 2000.

06 — The Big Picture

Hotter, drier, and more dangerous

Taken together, the data tells a clear and consistent story. As Australia's temperatures have risen, rainfall has declined and extreme events have multiplied. The scatter plot below directly links the two primary datasets — confirming that hotter years tend to be drier years. The strip plot shows the dramatic rightward shift in temperature distribution decade by decade, leaving little room for doubt about the direction of change.

Key finding: The regression line in the scatter plot shows a statistically meaningful negative relationship between temperature anomaly and annual rainfall — as temperatures rise, rainfall falls. Each dot is coloured by decade, revealing how recent decades (2000s and 2010s, shown in warm colours) cluster in the high-temperature, low-rainfall corner.
